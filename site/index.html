The philosophy

Their best performances should come years from now. We program like it.

Grounded in the long-term athletic development consensus (NSCA position stand, the Youth Physical Development model) and built by a coach who works with young athletes every week.

Sprint. Jump. Lift. Every age.

The chassis never changes: sprint 2-4 times a week, jump in all three planes, lift from bodyweight up. What changes is the dose. A trained 8-year-old and a trained 13-year-old run the same structure at very different volumes.

Movement quality opens doors.

Output never outranks control. An athlete who can't stick a single-leg landing does not get depth drops, no matter how strong or fast they are. The screen's hard gates route every athlete to work they're ready to win.

Growth spurts change the plan.

Height is tracked quarterly and growth velocity is computed, because the spurt is measurable, not eyeballed. Near peak growth, the plan automatically eases volume and doubles down on landing quality. Pull back, don't push through.

No gimmicks. No gassers.

Flying sprints instead of 60-yard flat-outs. Quality reps instead of stopwatch chasing. Weekly sport-plus-training hours capped near the child's age, 1-2 real rest days, and 9-10 hours of sleep counted as training.

From a 20-minute screen to a season of right-sized training.

1

Screen

Six simple tests: squat, drop-and-stick landing, single-leg balance, push-ups, broad jump, pogo hops. Each scored 0-3 in about 20 minutes, no lab required.

2

Place

The engine turns those scores into a training placement, with safety gates that route athletes toward work their movement quality has earned.

3

Train

Complete 2, 3, or 4-day weekly sessions, assembled from a 127-exercise library with doses, rest, and coaching cues. Blocks rotate so training never goes stale.

See the placement engine think.

These are the real rules. Score every test a 3 and drop just the landing to a 2: a 17-out-of-18 athlete still gets held back from the highest-impact work, because landing control outranks everything.

Coaches see the full picture. Athletes and parents see what to work on next, never a grade.
